Integration of Eulerian Eikonal Solvers and Isosurface Extraction into Finslerian Deep Learning Frameworks1. Introduction to Finslerian Propagation Models and the Holonomic Association ModelThe modeling of wavefront propagation in heterogeneous, anisotropic media has undergone a profound transformation with the advent of differentiable geometric solvers and deep learning architectures. Within the context of computational physics and spatial modeling, the Holonomic Association Model (HAMtools) has established a robust, highly performant foundation for Finslerian deep learning. By treating the geometric metric tensor as a learnable neural module rather than a static analytical formula, the framework parameterizes the propagation environment using the Zermelo navigation problem. This formulation results in a Randers-Finsler metric that elegantly and rigorously captures both the anisotropic nature of topographies and the asymmetric influence of external forces, such as wind drift. Currently, the primary computational engine within HAMtools relies on a Lagrangian approach, specifically utilizing the Augmented Vertex Block Descent (AVBD) algorithm to resolve boundary-value problems and an explicit Runge-Kutta integration of the geodesic spray for initial-value problems.While the Lagrangian formulation provides explicit geodesic paths and enables advanced geometric analyses—such as Jacobi field divergence mapping and curvature anomaly detection—it faces severe computational bottlenecks when applied to dense spatial domains. Resolving the arrival time for an entire landscape requires solving individual boundary-value problems for every spatial coordinate, rendering full-field evaluation computationally prohibitive and introducing statistical noise during neural network training. In contrast, recent advancements in Eulerian formulations, notably the differentiable eikonal solver introduced by Gahtan et al., demonstrate that the Fast Sweeping Method (FSM) can compute exact distance fields over Cartesian grids in linear time. By employing implicit differentiation on the converged partial differential equation, the Eulerian approach circumvents the memory constraints of unrolled ordinary differential equation integration, offering exact gradients for metric recovery without exhausting the high-bandwidth memory (HBM) of modern accelerators.The integration of such an Eulerian solver into a generalized manifold framework requires extending the fast sweeping paradigm from regular Cartesian grids to unstructured triangular meshes. Furthermore, generalizing the propagation model from a two-dimensional surface constraint to a fully three-dimensional volumetric grid introduces profound topological complexities in wavefront reconstruction. Algorithms traditionally utilized in computer graphics and game development, such as Marching Cubes, provide a direct, computationally efficient mechanism for extracting isochrones—temporal isosurfaces—from three-dimensional distance fields. The subsequent analysis provides a comprehensive mathematical comparison of these paradigms, explores the extension of eikonal solvers to arbitrary manifolds, evaluates the critical role of differentiable isosurface extraction in volumetric grids, and delineates a precise, phase-by-phase implementation strategy for unifying these capabilities within the HAMtools JAX architecture.2. Mathematical Duality: Lagrangian Geodesics versus Eulerian Eikonal FrameworksThe fundamental objective in wavefront propagation, particularly in the context of wildfire spread modeling over complex terrains, is to determine the minimum arrival time from a source ignition point to any spatial coordinate within the domain. This problem can be approached from two mathematically dual perspectives: tracking the trajectories of individual particles along optimal paths (the Lagrangian approach) or tracking the evolution of the continuous wavefront scalar field across the entire domain (the Eulerian approach). Understanding the deep mathematical connections and practical divergences between these two formulations is essential for designing optimal learning architectures.2.1. The Lagrangian Formulation and Geodesic Spray DynamicsIn the Lagrangian perspective currently employed by the HAMtools architecture, propagation is governed by the minimization of the Finsler energy functional. Given a differentiable manifold equipped with a Randers metric, the cost function is defined by a combination of a Riemannian base metric tensor and a drift vector field. The length of a path is the integral of this cost function over the parameterization of the curve. Minimizing this action functional via the calculus of variations yields the Euler-Lagrange equations, which can be elegantly expressed in terms of the geodesic spray. The spray dictates the second-order ordinary differential equation governing the particle's motion, encapsulating the inertial flow of the wavefront across the manifold.To determine the arrival time at a specific coordinate using this paradigm, a boundary-value problem must be solved to find the minimizing trajectory connecting the source to the target target. The AVBD solver within HAMtools discretizes this trajectory into a sequence of vertices and iteratively adjusts them via block coordinate descent on the manifold to minimize the discrete action. While this explicitly yields the fastest path—which is highly valuable for analyzing the physical corridors of fire spread and understanding terrain channeling effects—the solver is intrinsically localized. It provides absolutely no information about the arrival times at spatial coordinates that do not reside on the optimized trajectory. Consequently, generating a dense arrival time map for a high-resolution grid requires an independent optimization process for every single pixel, scaling the computational complexity by a factor of the grid size and rendering the approach impractical for full-scene supervision during deep learning.Furthermore, the Lagrangian approach struggles with topological ambiguities inherent in heterogeneous media. When a wavefront encounters an obstacle or propagates through a highly variable fuel bed, the characteristic rays may intersect, forming what is mathematically known as a cut locus. A boundary-value solver initialized with a naive straight-line path may easily converge to a local minimum, corresponding to a geodesic that is valid locally but is not the global minimizer of the arrival time. This failure to capture the global minimum directly biases the loss landscape, misguiding the neural metric encoder during the backpropagation phase.2.2. The Eulerian Formulation and the Hamilton-Jacobi EquationThe Eulerian perspective defines a continuous scalar field representing the minimum arrival time at every point in the domain simultaneously. The fundamental relationship between the Lagrangian cost function and the Eulerian arrival time field is governed by the Legendre transform, which maps the velocity-dependent Lagrangian into a momentum-dependent Hamiltonian.For a propagating wavefront, the arrival time field must satisfy the static Hamilton-Jacobi equation, commonly referred to as the Eikonal equation. For a Randers-Finsler metric, the co-metric applied to the gradient of the arrival time field must equal unity, effectively stating that the magnitude of the gradient of the arrival time is inversely proportional to the local propagation speed. The characteristics of this partial differential equation are precisely the minimizing geodesics of the Lagrangian formulation, establishing a strict mathematical duality in the smooth, continuous regime. Solving the eikonal equation globally over the domain yields the minimum distance from the source to every coordinate in a single computational pass.The Eulerian approach naturally and robustly handles the topological complexities that challenge Lagrangian solvers. Numerical schemes designed for the eikonal equation, such as the Fast Sweeping Method and the Fast Marching Method, compute the unique viscosity solution of the partial differential equation. The viscosity solution explicitly selects the globally minimizing path at every point, inherently encoding the cut locus and seamlessly managing the merging, splitting, and colliding of wavefronts without requiring complex multi-start initializations or heuristics. This ensures that the arrival times used to compute the training loss reflect the true physical propagation of the simulated fire front, providing a pristine gradient signal to the neural network.2.3. Autodifferentiation Paradigms and Computational ComplexityThe transition from forward physical simulation to inverse metric recovery necessitates computing the gradient of the arrival time with respect to the underlying metric parameters, such as the spatial covariates and the weights of the neural encoder. The choice between the Lagrangian and Eulerian solvers dictates the feasible automatic differentiation strategies, heavily impacting the memory profile and execution speed on hardware accelerators like GPUs and TPUs.In the Lagrangian framework, gradients are typically obtained by unrolling the iterations of the numerical integrator—such as the Runge-Kutta steps for the initial-value problem or the Gauss-Seidel vertex updates in the AVBD algorithm—and applying standard reverse-mode automatic differentiation. This algorithmic unrolling requires storing the intermediate states of the trajectory at every integration step in the computation graph, resulting in a memory footprint that scales linearly with both the number of steps and the number of optimized paths. For high-resolution wildfire domains spanning thousands of pixels, or for long-horizon propagation simulations, this memory overhead becomes a severely limiting factor, drastically restricting the permissible batch size during neural network training and introducing latency due to excessive memory allocation operations.The Eulerian framework, specifically via the methodology presented by Gahtan et al., leverages the implicit function theorem on the converged discrete system to bypass the unrolling completely. Because the Fast Sweeping Method utilizes upwind finite differences, the arrival time at any given node depends exclusively on neighboring nodes with strictly smaller arrival times. This strict causality induces a directed acyclic graph over the spatial grid. At convergence, the discrete residual of the update equations is identically zero. Differentiating this residual with respect to the metric parameters yields an adjoint linear system. Due to the causal ordering of the nodes, the Jacobian of the residual with respect to the arrival times forms a sparse, lower-triangular matrix. The adjoint system can thus be solved exactly and efficiently via a single reverse-time back-substitution pass. Extending State of the Art Eulerian Solvers to Unstructured ManifoldsThe implementation of the differentiable eikonal solver by Gahtan et al. is strictly constrained to regular, two-dimensional Cartesian grids. While this configuration is highly efficient and easily vectorized for flat topographic projections, this limitation severely restricts the geometric fidelity of the simulation, particularly when modeling propagation over the highly irregular topologies characteristic of mountainous wildfire scenarios. The HAMtools library intrinsically supports operations on arbitrary manifolds, specifically emphasizing TriangularMesh domains embedded in three-dimensional space to capture true surface distances rather than projected planar distances. Unifying the Eulerian efficiency with this geometric flexibility requires adapting the Fast Sweeping Method to unstructured triangulations.3.1. Fast Sweeping Dynamics on Unstructured TriangulationsThe Cartesian Fast Sweeping Method relies fundamentally on the natural, axis-aligned ordering of grid nodes to cover all possible characteristic directions through a set of alternating sweeping passes (e.g., four sweeps in two dimensions, eight sweeps in three dimensions). On an unstructured triangular mesh, this natural geometric ordering does not exist, and characteristics can propagate in arbitrary directions relative to the mesh edges. Adapting the fast sweeping method requires establishing a systematic sequencing of the vertices to ensure that information propagates causally along the characteristics, regardless of the underlying mesh topology.The standard mathematical approach for unstructured meshes, established extensively in the literature by Kao, Osher, and Qian, dictates the introduction of multiple reference points strategically distributed across the manifold. The vertices of the triangular mesh are subsequently sorted based on their shortest-path distance (typically using an $L_1$ or $L_2$ metric proxy) to each of these reference points, generating a series of ascending and descending ordered lists. A complete sweeping iteration consists of traversing the mesh according to these precomputed orderings in sequence. By strategically placing reference points—such as at the extrema of the domain or uniformly distributed across the surface—the resulting sweeps mathematically guarantee that every possible characteristic direction is covered. This ensures that the upwind information flow is properly resolved across the manifold and that the algorithm converges in a finite, small number of iterations, restoring the $O(N)$ efficiency of the Cartesian method.3.2. Causality and the Godunov Numerical HamiltonianThe computational core of the eikonal solver is the local update rule applied at each vertex. For a vertex situated in a triangular mesh, the arrival time must be computed based on the known values of its adjacent neighbors. This computation is strictly governed by the Godunov numerical Hamiltonian, which provides a monotone, upwind finite-difference approximation of the spatial gradient that respects the physics of wave propagation.Given a triangular element defined by vertices $A$, $B$, and $C$, where the arrival times at $A$ and $B$ have already been established by the sweeping process, the objective is to determine the optimal arrival time at $C$. The local solver operates under the assumption that the wavefront propagates linearly across the element. The mathematical validity of the update depends entirely on the causality condition: the characteristic ray extending from $C$ backward to the propagating wavefront must lie strictly within the interior of the triangle $ABC$. If the characteristic falls outside the geometric boundaries of the triangle, it indicates that the physical information is arriving from a different direction, and the update from this specific element must be rejected to prevent non-physical artifacts.This strict geometric constraint introduces severe complications on highly irregular meshes containing obtuse angles. If the angle at vertex $C$ is obtuse, the characteristic ray may trace back outside the triangle even when $A$ and $B$ are the physically correct upwind donors. To preserve monotonicity and guarantee algorithmic convergence, the mesh must undergo a rigorous preprocessing phase where virtual edges are introduced to dynamically partition obtuse angles into acute components. Once the mesh satisfies this global acute-angle criterion, the Godunov local solver can robustly determine the arrival time at $C$ by evaluating the quadratic equation derived from the local metric tensor. The solver systematically processes all triangles adjacent to $C$ and assigns the minimum valid arrival time, thereby strictly enforcing the causality principle and ensuring the viscosity solution is recovered.3.3. Leveraging Existing JAX Paradigms for Mesh-Based Eikonal SolversThe implementation of these sophisticated algorithms within a compiled, differentiable framework is non-trivial due to the dynamic branching inherent in stencil selection, angle unfolding, and causality checking. However, the viability of this approach in JAX has been definitively established by recent open-source libraries such as Eikonax and FIMJAX.These specialized libraries successfully implement Godunov-type upwinding on triangulated meshes while retaining full compatibility with JAX's Just-In-Time (JIT) compilation and automatic differentiation. By utilizing continuous approximations or carefully structuring the jax.lax.cond control flow mechanisms to avoid XLA compilation failures, they compute the exact parametric derivatives of the arrival time with respect to the anisotropic metric tensor. Specifically, Eikonax utilizes causality-informed discrete adjoints to evaluate the vector-Jacobian products, perfectly echoing the exact implicit differentiation strategy proposed by Gahtan et al., but elevated to unstructured manifolds.Integrating a comparable module into the HAMtools architecture provides a direct pathway to state-of-the-art Eulerian simulation. By encapsulating the reference-point sorting and the Godunov numerical Hamiltonian within an Equinox neural module, HAMtools can execute full-field distance computations over authentic terrain meshes, seamlessly backpropagating the gradients through the adjoint linear system to update the neural metric encoder without requiring external PyTorch or Numba dependencies.4. Generalizing to Three-Dimensional Grids and Differentiable Wavefront ExtractionWhile two-dimensional surface modeling is sufficient for rudimentary wildfire tracking along the ground, accurate physical simulations frequently necessitate an expansion into three-dimensional volumetric spaces. Fire propagation involves complex volumetric fuel beds, vertical atmospheric interactions, and crucial topological variations such as crown fires that ignite above the surface topography and radiate heat downwards. Solving the eikonal equation on a three-dimensional Cartesian grid provides the volumetric arrival time field, but visualizing, penalizing, and utilizing this data requires extracting the physical propagating wavefront at specific temporal intervals.4.1. The Role of Marching Cubes in Volumetric Eikonal SolversThe Marching Cubes algorithm serves as the critical mathematical bridge between the implicit Eulerian distance field and explicit geometric representations. When the eikonal solver processes a three-dimensional grid, it assigns a scalar arrival time to each voxel corner, generating a dense volume of temporal data. To determine the physical location of the fire front at a specific continuous time (the isochrone), one must extract the corresponding isosurface, which represents the zero-level set of the time-shifted distance field.Marching Cubes operates by iterating over every cubic cell in the volumetric grid. By evaluating the scalar arrival time at the eight vertices of a cube, the algorithm determines which vertices lie behind the propagating wavefront (arrival time less than the target time) and which lie ahead (arrival time greater than the target time). This binary classification yields a specific topological state for the cell, out of 256 possible permutations. The algorithm utilizes a precomputed lookup table to map this state to a specific configuration of triangles. The exact vertices of these triangles are then placed along the edges of the cube via linear interpolation based on the scalar arrival time values, resulting in a continuous, triangulated mesh representing the precise location of the fire front in three-dimensional space.4.2. From Implicit Arrival Times to Physical ManifoldsThe integration of Marching Cubes directly complements the Eulerian eikonal approach, establishing a unified pipeline for physical simulation. While the eikonal solver provides the governing physics of the propagation, Marching Cubes provides the geometric extraction necessary for downstream tasks. For instance, computing the interaction between the fire front and atmospheric wind models requires the explicit surface normal vectors of the wavefront. Because the eikonal equation mathematically dictates that the gradient of the arrival time field is orthogonal to the wavefront, the surface normals required by the Marching Cubes algorithm can be computed directly and analytically from the spatial derivatives of the eikonal solution, bypassing noisy finite-difference approximations on the extracted mesh.Furthermore, extending the framework to three dimensions mitigates the geometric distortions inherent in projecting rugged terrain onto a two-dimensional grid. A flat grid approximation fundamentally miscalculates surface distances on steep slopes, scaling errors by the inverse cosine of the incline angle, which drastically corrupts arrival time predictions. Differentiability Constraints in Isosurface ExtractionTo maintain end-to-end learnability within the neural framework, the wavefront extraction process must rigorously preserve gradient flow. Standard implementations of Marching Cubes rely on discrete lookup tables and hard thresholding, which are fundamentally non-differentiable operations that block backpropagation. However, because the precise vertex placement along the cube edges is determined by linear interpolation of the arrival times, the geometric positions of the extracted mesh vertices are continuously differentiable with respect to the eikonal scalar field.To backpropagate a loss defined on the extracted wavefront—such as a point-to-surface distance penalty against a ground-truth satellite fire perimeter—back to the underlying metric parameters, the system must track the dependence of the interpolated vertex coordinates on the voxel arrival times. If the spatial topology changes, such as when a wavefront splits around a non-combustible obstacle like a lake, the discrete configuration of the Marching Cubes cell changes abruptly. While this introduces a point of non-differentiability at the exact moment of topological transition, the solution remains piecewise differentiable over the vast majority of the domain. In practice, utilizing differentiable variants of isosurface extraction ensures that spatial losses evaluated on the extracted fire perimeters provide stable, non-zero gradients to the neural metric encoder, enabling the optimization of the three-dimensional propagation environment.5. Strategic Benefits of Integrating an Eikonal Solver into HAMtoolsThe incorporation of a differentiable eikonal solver fundamentally enhances the capabilities of the HAMtools library, resolving critical evaluation bottlenecks, aligning the methodology with state-of-the-art research, and expanding the scope of feasible inverse problems.5.1. Resolving Observation Density Asymmetries and Training PathologiesThe most pressing limitation of the current Lagrangian approach in HAMtools is the massive asymmetry between the available observation density and the evaluation protocol. Wildfire tracking datasets, such as Sim2Real-Fire, provide high-resolution satellite imagery indicating the temporal progression of the fire mask over tens of thousands of pixels. To evaluate the correlation between predicted and observed arrival times, and to compute critical metrics like Intersection over Union (IoU), predictions must be generated for the entire burned area.Because the AVBD solver computes individual paths, evaluating the entire perimeter requires solving tens of thousands of independent boundary-value problems. Consequently, HAMtools currently evaluates the loss and the correlation metrics on a severely subsampled set of target points. This sparsity introduces immense statistical noise, fundamentally compromises the correlation metric, and prevents the framework from fairly benchmarking against Eulerian methods that natively output dense fields. The integration of the eikonal solver rectifies this pathology immediately. A single forward pass of the fast sweeping algorithm yields the arrival time at every node in the mesh. This allows the loss function to be evaluated over the complete, un-sampled satellite fire mask, providing a dense, high-fidelity gradient signal to the neural network and enabling exact, apples-to-apples comparative benchmarking against Gahtan et al..5.2. Complementing Geodesic Path Extraction and Geometric AnalysisCrucially, the inclusion of the eikonal solver does not render the existing Lagrangian solvers obsolete; rather, they form a symbiotic, mutually reinforcing geometric toolset. The Eulerian solver is mathematically optimal for global distance computation and dense metric recovery during the training phase. Once the neural metric is trained and the complex environment is parameterized, the Lagrangian AVBD and Initial Value Problem solvers can be deployed to extract profound geometric insights that are inaccessible to Eulerian models alone.For instance, identifying the optimal deployment routes for firefighting resources requires explicit, connected trajectories, which the Eulerian field does not natively provide. The AVBD solver can instantly extract these minimal-energy corridors across the trained metric landscape. Furthermore, evaluating the physical stability of the fire front relies on measuring the divergence of adjacent characteristics. The Lagrangian framework naturally supports the computation of Jacobi fields and the evaluation of Finslerian curvature along the specified paths, allowing analysts to identify regions of severe wind shear or fuel discontinuity that threaten to trigger erratic, highly dangerous fire behavior. By combining the dense training capability of the eikonal solver with the explicit geometric interpretation of the Lagrangian solvers, the HAMtools framework achieves unparalleled analytical depth, positioning it as the definitive tool for state-of-the-art research in physics-based deep learning.6.
